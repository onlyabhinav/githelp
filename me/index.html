Chapter One

The Gap Between Intent and Impact

Most workplace conflict is not a disagreement about the work. It is a disagreement about how the work was said. You can be entirely right and still lose the room — because the room is listening for respect before it listens for reason.

A neutral sentence, delivered at the wrong moment or with the wrong weight, can land as sarcasm. A correction offered publicly — even gently — can read as a humiliation. The content was never the problem. The packaging was.

The first discipline, then, is noticing that what you meant and what they received are two different objects. Intent lives in your head. Impact lives in theirs. Leadership is the work of closing that gap.

You handled the content correctly. Your communication style is what needs refinement. — The lesson, distilled

Chapter Two

The Professional Buffer

Blunt isn’t wrong; blunt is unbuffered. The simplest upgrade to tone is a three-beat formula you can apply on the fly, in any meeting, in any language.

Formula

Acknowledge · State · Suggest

First make the other person feel heard. Then make your point. Then open a door they can walk through.

acknowledge → state → suggest
✕ Blunt

This doesn’t make sense.

✓ Buffered

I’m not fully clear on this part — can we walk through it once?

✕ Blunt

You’re not letting me speak.

✓ Buffered

Let me quickly complete my point, then I’d like to hear your view.

✕ Blunt

I already told you this.

✓ Buffered

I think we touched on this earlier — maybe we can align again.

✕ Blunt

You said ten seconds but it’s been two minutes.

✓ Buffered

Can I jump in once you wrap this thought?

Chapter Three

Phrases to Retire

Five phrases do more damage than their size suggests. Remove them from active vocabulary and replace them with five quieter alternatives. This single swap, repeated for a month, changes how people experience you.

✕

I already told you.

✓

Maybe we can align on this once more.

✕

This is wrong.

✓

I might be missing something, but…

✕

You are not understanding.

✓

Can we look at it this way?

✕

This is obvious.

✓

Just to confirm our shared understanding…

✕

You didn’t do this.

✓

One suggestion here — we might want to add…

You don’t need to become soft. You need to become clear, respectful, and structured.

Chapter Four

The Meeting Script

When the pressure is live, you won’t invent language — you will reach for whatever is already on the shelf. Put these on the shelf. Read them until they feel like your own voice.

To enter a conversation
  • Can I add something here?
  • Just building on that point…
  • Let me share my understanding.
To disagree
  • I see your point, though one concern is…
  • That makes sense — another angle could be…
  • I might be missing something, but I see it differently.
When something is unclear
  • I’m not fully clear on this — can you walk me through it?
  • Just to confirm my understanding…
  • Can we clarify how this works in implementation?
When interrupted
  • Let me quickly complete, then I’ll come to you.
  • Ten seconds — I’ll wrap this up.
When someone monologues
  • Can I jump in once you wrap this thought?
  • Let me add one point here.
To give feedback
  • One suggestion here…
  • We might want to add more detail around…
  • From a developer perspective, it would help if…
When going off-track
  • Maybe we can take this offline and stay on the main agenda?
  • Let’s park this for now and come back if needed.
To close your point
  • That’s my view — happy to hear others.
  • Let me pause here and get your thoughts.
The emergency neutralizer
  • Maybe I’m missing something here…

Use when tension rises. It reduces conflict instantly without surrendering your authority.

Cheat Card

Start soft · Say your point · End open

“Just adding here…” → [your logic] → “What do you think?”

Chapter Five

From Person to Process

When the same conversation keeps repeating itself, the problem is no longer the conversation. It is the absence of structure around it. You can argue with a person forever. You cannot argue with a checklist.

Shift the frame: stop trying to make them understand, and start making expectations explicit and documented. This is the single move that separates a senior engineer from a tech lead, and a tech lead from a manager.

Clarity in documentation avoids conflict in discussion.

The instinct, when frustrated, is to take over — to write the story yourself, to do the BA’s job. Resist it. Taking over produces burnout, role confusion, and no long-term fix. The leverage is elsewhere: in creating a structure that makes the gap self-evident, without you ever having to name it.

Chapter Six

A Readiness Gate

A Definition of Ready is not bureaucracy. It is a quiet, unarguable standard that turns personal friction into shared process. Nothing enters development until the checklist is green — no negotiation needed, because the rule was agreed to in advance.

Definition of Ready · Tap to check

  • Business context is defined
  • Acceptance criteria are complete (Given / When / Then)
  • At least two example scenarios are provided
  • Expected input and output are clearly defined
  • Edge cases are identified
  • Dependencies are listed
  • No open functional questions remain
Standard Response

When a story isn’t ready

“From a development perspective, this story is not yet ready. Missing: example scenario, expected output, edge-case handling. Once these are added, we can proceed.”

No emotion. No argument. Just the standard.

When asked to start anyway: “Sure, I can begin with assumptions — but there’s a risk of rework. Would you like me to proceed, or should we clarify first?” This is not refusal. It is shifted responsibility, and it is how accountability is made visible without confrontation.

Chapter Seven

On Acting Like an Owner

You don’t need the title. You need the posture. A tech lead is not a manager in miniature — it is the person who ensures the work is implementable, who reduces ambiguity, who protects the team from rework, and who builds structure where structure is missing.

Ordering people, evaluating performance, fixing personalities — those belong to someone else. Clarity, process, delivery quality — those belong to you. The shift is not about authority; it is about ownership.

The Shift

From solving problems to preventing them

Senior Engineer

Solves problems · Reacts · Focus on code

Tech Lead

Prevents problems · Designs process · Focus on delivery

Am I making delivery smoother, or harder? — The only question that matters

Chapter Eight

A Case in Miniature

One team discussion about story detail went sideways. Studying it closely reveals the whole architecture of modern workplace conflict — and the specific corrections that would have prevented it.

Trigger
A literal observation — “you said ten seconds but continued speaking” — landed as sarcasm in front of the team.
Escalation
The discussion shifted from what is right to how it was said. Both sides began fighting for control of the floor.
Public turn
One party took control of the call and asked others to leave — making a private tension into a public incident.
Underneath
This was never about one sentence. It was accumulated frustration meeting the first available spark.
The correction
Not a better argument. A better script, a 1:1 alignment afterwards, and a structural fix (the readiness gate) so the underlying friction couldn’t recur.
The damage control
“I think things got a bit heated. My intention was not to sound sarcastic — if it came across that way, I understand your concern.” Don’t defend. Align. Then move the conversation from behavior back to work.

Chapter Nine

Principles, Kept Short

Read before meetings. Read after difficult ones. These are the ones that fit on a single card.

  1. Intent is not impact. What you meant lives in your head. What they heard lives in theirs. Leadership is closing that gap, not defending your intent.
  2. Never correct tone or timing in public. It always escalates. Always. Take it to a 1:1, and take it there calmly.
  3. Don’t defend. Align. After a flare-up, the first move is acknowledgment, not justification. “If it came across that way, I understand.”
  4. Ask questions instead of declaring errors. A question invites. A declaration accuses. Reach for “Can we clarify…?” before “This is wrong.”
  5. Shift from person to process. When the same problem recurs, stop debating the person and start designing the structure that makes the problem visible.
  6. Put things in writing. Verbal agreements evaporate. A short written summary after every discussion creates accountability without confrontation.
  7. Own the outcome, not the people. A tech lead is a delivery owner, not a miniature manager. You don’t manage personalities; you remove ambiguity.
  8. Package your intelligence. Being right is not enough. The same idea, better delivered, will travel further. Lower the volume; raise the clarity.
  9. Start soft, end open. Open with “Just adding here…”, close with “What do you think?”. Authority survives intact; ego dissolves.
  10. When in doubt, say: “Maybe I’m missing something, but…” One phrase, infinite uses. It opens the door, lowers the temperature, and never costs you an inch of ground.

Chapter Ten

Further Reading

Ten books, chosen carefully — not a wall of recommendations, but the ones that actually change how you handle Monday morning. Each is annotated with why it earns a place on the shelf.

Conversation

Crucial Conversations

Patterson, Grenny, McMillan & Switzler

The definitive manual for high-stakes talks. Teaches how to stay in dialogue when stakes are high, opinions differ, and emotions run strong. Directly addresses the moment when a team discussion turns personal.

Difficult feedback

Thanks for the Feedback

Douglas Stone & Sheila Heen

Explains why even well-meant feedback triggers defensiveness, and how to receive it without collapsing or arguing. Pair with Difficult Conversations by the same authors.

Management

The Manager’s Path

Camille Fournier

Written for engineers. Covers the transition from senior engineer to tech lead to manager — with specific chapters on ownership, influence without authority, and the hardest conversations.

Direct leadership

Radical Candor

Kim Scott

Care personally, challenge directly. Gives a vocabulary for being honest without being harsh, and explains the trap of ruinous empathy — softening so much the message never lands.

Listening

Nonviolent Communication

Marshall B. Rosenberg

A framework for expressing needs and hearing others without blame or judgment. Changes how you diagnose what a conflict is actually about, which is usually not what it appears to be.

Negotiation

Never Split the Difference

Chris Voss

Tactical empathy from a former FBI negotiator. The techniques — labelling, mirroring, calibrated questions — translate directly to standups, 1:1s, and any moment someone is being defensive.

Emotional clarity

Search Inside Yourself

Chade-Meng Tan

Emotional intelligence for engineers, from a Google engineer. Pragmatic, unmystical, and practical. Teaches the pause between stimulus and response — the space where tone is chosen.

Identity & habit

Atomic Habits

James Clear

Changing communication is not a decision — it is a habit. This book explains how to design your environment so new patterns (swapping one blunt phrase a day) compound into a new default.

Leadership presence

The Making of a Manager

Julie Zhuo

Honest, field-level writing from a Facebook VP. Covers the subtle shifts — running meetings, giving feedback, building trust — that distinguish a good lead from a great one.

Thinking clearly

Thinking, Fast and Slow

Daniel Kahneman

Not a leadership book, but the foundation under every leadership book. Understanding your own cognitive biases is the first step to noticing when you’re reacting, not thinking.

Agile delivery

User Story Mapping

Jeff Patton

The book to hand a BA (or read yourself) when documentation quality is the bottleneck. Teaches how to structure stories so implementation is obvious — fixing the system, not the person.

Philosophy · brief

Meditations

Marcus Aurelius

A Roman emperor’s private notebook on self-discipline, patience, and dealing with difficult colleagues. Two thousand years old, still the best short read on keeping composure under provocation.
